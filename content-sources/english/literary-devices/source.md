# English Literary Devices: High School Source Notes

## Purpose

These notes ground the first Learn Anything content drop for English Literary Devices. The target learner is a high-school student who needs to recognize common literary devices, explain their effects, and apply the concepts to short examples.

The first learning path should include one intro lesson and one 15-question mixed multiple-choice quiz. The quiz should include both recognition and application questions.

## Learning goals

By the end of the intro lesson and practice quiz, learners should be able to:

- define ten common literary devices
- identify a device in a sentence or short passage
- distinguish commonly confused devices
- explain the effect a device creates for a reader
- apply a device concept to a new example

## Devices in scope

### Metaphor

A metaphor compares one thing to another by saying that one thing is another thing. It does not use "like" or "as."

Example: "The classroom was a furnace by the end of the day."

Effect: A metaphor creates a vivid connection between two ideas. It can make an abstract feeling or situation easier to picture.

Common confusion: A metaphor is different from a simile because a simile uses "like" or "as" to make the comparison.

### Simile

A simile compares two things using "like" or "as."

Example: "Her explanation was as clear as glass."

Effect: A simile helps the reader understand one thing by comparing it to something familiar.

Common confusion: Not every sentence with "like" or "as" is a simile. The words must create a comparison.

### Personification

Personification gives human qualities, actions, or feelings to something nonhuman.

Example: "The wind whispered through the empty hallway."

Effect: Personification can make a scene feel more alive, emotional, or dramatic.

Common confusion: Personification is not just describing an object. The object, animal, idea, or natural force must be given a human trait or action.

### Imagery

Imagery uses descriptive language that appeals to the senses: sight, sound, smell, taste, or touch.

Example: "The sharp scent of rain rose from the warm pavement."

Effect: Imagery helps readers picture or experience a scene more vividly.

Common confusion: Imagery is not limited to visual description. It can involve any of the five senses.

### Alliteration

Alliteration repeats the same beginning consonant sound in nearby words.

Example: "Bright banners billowed beside the bridge."

Effect: Alliteration can create rhythm, emphasis, mood, or memorability.

Common confusion: Alliteration is based on sound, not spelling. "Cycle" and "cat" do not alliterate because their starting sounds differ.

### Hyperbole

Hyperbole is an intentional exaggeration used for emphasis or effect.

Example: "I have told you a thousand times to check your work."

Effect: Hyperbole can show strong emotion, humor, frustration, or intensity.

Common confusion: Hyperbole is not meant to be taken literally. The exaggeration is obvious or deliberate.

### Irony

Irony involves a contrast between what is expected and what actually happens, or between what is said and what is meant.

Example: A fire station burns down during a safety inspection.

Effect: Irony can create humor, surprise, criticism, or deeper meaning.

Common confusion: Irony is not simply any bad luck or coincidence. There must be a meaningful contrast between expectation and reality.

### Symbolism

Symbolism uses an object, character, place, color, or action to represent a larger idea.

Example: A locked door may symbolize lost opportunity or separation.

Effect: Symbolism adds deeper meaning beyond the literal details of a text.

Common confusion: A symbol must stand for something beyond itself. Not every important object in a story is automatically a symbol.

### Foreshadowing

Foreshadowing gives hints or clues about what may happen later in a text.

Example: A character notices dark clouds gathering before an important outdoor ceremony.

Effect: Foreshadowing builds suspense, prepares the reader, and makes later events feel connected.

Common confusion: Foreshadowing is different from a flashback. Foreshadowing points forward; a flashback looks backward.

### Onomatopoeia

Onomatopoeia uses words that imitate sounds.

Example: "The bacon sizzled in the pan."

Effect: Onomatopoeia makes writing more vivid by helping the reader hear the action.

Common confusion: Not every sound-related word is onomatopoeia. The word should imitate or strongly suggest the sound itself.

## Common comparison points

Metaphor vs. simile:

- Metaphor: says one thing is another thing.
- Simile: compares using "like" or "as."

Imagery vs. symbolism:

- Imagery: helps readers sense or picture something.
- Symbolism: uses something concrete to represent a larger idea.

Foreshadowing vs. irony:

- Foreshadowing: hints at what may happen later.
- Irony: creates contrast between expectation and reality or meaning.

Alliteration vs. onomatopoeia:

- Alliteration: repeated beginning consonant sounds.
- Onomatopoeia: words that imitate sounds.

Hyperbole vs. metaphor:

- Hyperbole: exaggerates for emphasis.
- Metaphor: compares by saying one thing is another.

## Quiz design notes

The first quiz should contain 15 multiple-choice questions.

Recommended mix:

- 8 recognition questions
- 7 application questions

Recognition questions should ask learners to identify the device used in a sentence or short passage.

Application questions should ask learners to choose the best explanation, effect, or example of a device.

Every question should include:

- one correct answer
- plausible distractors
- a short explanation after submission
- a device tag
- a question type tag: recognition or application
- a difficulty label

Difficulty guidance:

- easy: direct examples with clear signal words or obvious effects
- medium: examples that require distinguishing two similar devices
- hard: short passages or subtler effects where the learner must infer the device or purpose

## Sample explanation style

Explanations should be brief, specific, and instructional.

Good: "This is personification because the wind is given the human action of whispering."

Avoid: "Correct. Personification is the right answer."

Good: "This is hyperbole because 'a thousand times' is an obvious exaggeration used to show frustration."

Avoid: "This sentence is dramatic."
